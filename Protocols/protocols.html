NEB Gibson Assembly Protocol (E5510)
Overview — the 3-step workflow
Gibson Assembly joins linearised DNA fragments that share overlapping sequences at their ends. The HiFi Master Mix contains an exonuclease (chews back 5′ ends to expose overlaps), a polymerase (fills gaps), and a ligase (seals nicks). The workflow is: (1) PCR-amplify vector and insert with primers that introduce the overlapping sequences → (2) DpnI digest to remove template plasmid → (3) assemble the purified fragments.
1 — Design overlapping primers (before PCR)
  1. Overlaps of 15–30 bp are optimal for NEBHiFi. Design your primers so the 3′ end anneals to your template (binding region, Tm ~60°C) and the 5′ overhang carries the overlap sequence matching the adjacent fragment.
  2. Check that overlap regions do not form strong secondary structure and avoid overlaps that fall on repetitive sequence.
  3. The vector is typically amplified by inverse PCR (back-to-back primers) to linearise it and add overhangs. The insert is amplified from its template with forward/reverse primers carrying the vector-matching overhangs.
Overlaps longer than 80 bp can reduce Gibson efficiency. Keep them 20–30 bp unless you have a specific reason for longer arms.
2 — PCR → DpnI → cleanup (one continuous workflow)
  1. Amplify vector backbone and insert separately using a high-fidelity polymerase (see calculator).
  2. Without purifying, add DpnI directly to the PCR tube — it's active in Q5 Master Mix buffer, no buffer exchange needed. Incubate 37°C × 60 min.
  3. Purify with a PCR cleanup column. Elute in 15–20 uL. Measure by NanoDrop — needed for the assembly calculator.
  4. Optional gel check: run 2–3 uL on a 1% gel to confirm band size and absence of non-specific products.
3 — Gibson assembly reaction
  1. Set up on ice. Add DNA fragments and water first; add NEBHiFi 2× Master Mix last.
  2. pmols = (ng × 1000) / (bp × 650) — aim for 0.06 pmol vector, 2× molar excess of each insert.
  3. Incubate at 50°C: 15 min (2–3 fragments) or 60 min (4–6 fragments).
  4. Store on ice or at −20°C. Transform 2 uL into competent cells.
Tip
If you get very few colonies but positive controls work, the most common causes are: (1) incomplete DpnI digestion letting template through, (2) re-circularisation of uncut vector — confirm linearisation by gel before assembly, (3) poor overlap design. White/blue screening isn't applicable here; always verify by colony PCR and Sanger sequencing.

Restriction Enzyme Cloning (NEB)

CalculatorMol Biology
What is restriction cloning? Restriction enzymes cut DNA at specific short sequences (recognition sites), leaving either blunt or sticky (overhanging) ends. Two compatible sticky ends can be ligated directionally. The workflow is: (1) digest both vector and insert with the same enzyme(s) → (2) optionally dephosphorylate vector → (3) gel-purify or column-clean → (4) ligate → (5) transform. Use NEBcloner to select compatible enzymes and buffers.
1 — Choose your enzymes
  1. Use NEBcloner to identify restriction sites in your vector and insert, and to check buffer compatibility if doing a double digest.
  2. Single digest: simple but can allow insert in both orientations — use if the insert is directional by sequence context, or if you will screen by colony PCR.
  3. Double digest: two different enzymes giving incompatible ends force directional cloning — preferred when possible. Confirm both enzymes are active in the same buffer. Most NEB enzymes are compatible with CutSmart or rCutSmart.
  4. Check for internal sites: confirm neither enzyme cuts within your insert or within the region you want to keep in the vector.
For double digests, confirm both enzymes have ≥75% activity in the chosen buffer. If not, do sequential digests with a column cleanup in between.
2 — Digest reaction
Standard: 1 µg DNA in 50 µL. Scale down to 20 µL for small amounts. Always add enzyme last, keep on ice.
  1. Assemble on ice in order: water → buffer → DNA → enzyme(s) last.
  2. Mix gently by pipetting (do NOT vortex); spin briefly.
  3. Incubate at the enzyme-specific temperature (most NEB enzymes: 37°C) for 60 min. Time-Saver enzymes (marked TS): 15 min is sufficient.
  4. Stop reaction: heat-inactivate at 65°C × 20 min (most HF enzymes) or 80°C × 20 min — check the NEB datasheet. Alternatively add loading dye and proceed directly to gel.
Not all enzymes are heat-inactivatable. Check the NEB datasheet — enzymes not HI (e.g. SmaI, NotI) must be removed by column cleanup before ligation.
3 — Gel verification and purification
  1. Run the entire digest on a 0.8–1% TAE agarose gel with a 1 kb ladder.
  2. Confirm bands at expected sizes. Gel-extract both vector and insert — this removes uncut and partially-cut DNA, a major source of background colonies. Elute in 15–20 µL. Measure by NanoDrop.
  3. Minimise UV exposure when excising bands — use long-wave UV (365 nm) or a blue-light transilluminator if available.
4 — Dephosphorylation of vector (recommended for single digests)
  1. Why: after a single digest, the linearised vector can re-ligate on itself (background colonies with no insert). Removing the 5′ phosphate groups with a phosphatase prevents re-ligation — only the insert (which retains its 5′ phosphates) can close the circle.
  2. Add 1 µL Quick CIP (NEB) or rSAP directly to the digested, gel-purified vector; incubate 37°C × 10 min.
  3. Heat-inactivate: 80°C × 2 min (Quick CIP) or 65°C × 5 min (rSAP).
  4. Proceed directly to ligation — no additional cleanup needed after CIP/SAP inactivation.
5 — Ligation
Molar ratio: 3:1 to 5:1 insert:vector (molar) is standard. More insert = more efficient ligation, but too much can inhibit.
  1. Assemble on ice: water → 10× T4 Ligase Buffer (thaw and vortex to dissolve ATP) → vector → insert → T4 DNA Ligase last.
  2. Standard ligation: 16°C × 16 h (overnight, cold room or PCR block). Good for difficult ligations (blunt ends, long fragments).
  3. Quick Ligation (NEB M2200): 25°C × 5–15 min. Convenient for routine sticky-end ligations.
  4. Heat-inactivate at 65°C × 10 min, then chill on ice.
  5. Transform 2–5 µL into competent cells (see Transformation protocol).
T4 Ligase Buffer contains ATP which degrades on freeze-thaw. Use fresh aliquots; avoid more than 3 freeze-thaw cycles. Old buffer is a common cause of failed ligations.

Transformation of Competent Cells (DH5α / NEB Stable)

ReferenceMol Biology
What is this? Chemical transformation introduces plasmid DNA into competent E. coli by briefly permeabilising the cell membrane with heat shock. Used to amplify plasmid DNA after cloning (Gibson, restriction ligation) or to propagate existing constructs. Recovery in rich media (SOC) before plating allows cells to express the antibiotic resistance gene before selection. Best done in the evening so plates incubate overnight.
Preparation
  1. Thaw competent E. coli on ice — DH5α: ~20–30 min; NEB Stable: 5–10 min.
  2. Pre-warm LB agar plates with appropriate antibiotic at 37°C.
Transformation
  1. Mix 1–5 µL plasmid DNA (10 pg–100 ng) with 25–50 µL competent cells; flick gently to mix.
  2. Incubate on ice 2 min.
  3. Heat shock: 42°C × 30–60 s (typically 45 s; 30 s for NEB Stable).
  4. Back on ice 2 min.
  5. Add 450 µL LB/SOC; incubate 37°C × 1 h at 200–250 rpm (30°C for lentiviral plasmids).
Plating
  1. Spin briefly; resuspend in 50 µL LB/SOC; spread all on pre-warmed selective plates.
  2. Incubate overnight 37°C (30°C for lentiviral).
Colony selection
  1. Pick single colonies into 5 mL LB + antibiotic (15 mL tube); incubate overnight 37°C, 150–200 rpm.

Miniprep — GeneJET (Thermo K0502/K0503)

ReferenceMol Biology
What is this? A miniprep isolates plasmid DNA from a small overnight E. coli culture (5 mL) using alkaline lysis followed by silica-column purification. The GeneJET kit is reliable for routine plasmid prep — typical yield 5–20 µg, elution in 50 µL. Use a midiprep or maxiprep for larger amounts (transfection-grade DNA, virus production).
GeneJET Miniprep Kit (Thermo) — All steps at RT; centrifuge ≥12,000 × g. Culture 5 mL overnight in LB + antibiotic.
1 — Harvest & lyse
  1. Centrifuge 5 mL culture at 8000 rpm (6800 × g) × 2 min; discard supernatant.
  2. Add 250 µL Resuspension Solution — vortex until pellet is fully resuspended.
  3. Add 250 µL Lysis Solution — invert gently 4–6× until clear; do not vortex.
  4. Add 350 µL Neutralization Solution — invert 4–6×; centrifuge 5 min to pellet debris.
2 — Bind, wash & elute
  1. Transfer supernatant to GeneJET Spin Column in collection tube; centrifuge 1 min; discard flow-through.
  2. Add 500 µL Wash Solution; centrifuge 30–60 s; discard flow-through. Repeat wash once.
  3. Centrifuge empty column 1 min to dry the membrane.
  4. Transfer column to a clean 1.5 mL tube; add 50 µL Elution Buffer (or nuclease-free H₂O, pre-warmed to 65°C for higher yield); incubate 2 min at RT; centrifuge 2 min.
Do not over-lyse — blue lysate turning clear is the correct endpoint. Over-inversion can shear chromosomal DNA into the eluate, reducing purity (260/230 ratio).

NucleoSpin Gel & PCR Clean-up (Macherey-Nagel)

ReferenceMol Biology
What is this? A silica-membrane spin column that removes salts, enzymes, primers, and buffer components from PCR reactions or gel slices, recovering purified DNA. Use the PCR cleanup mode for clean PCR products (e.g. after DpnI digest). Use the gel extraction mode when you need to isolate a specific band away from other fragments or uncut vector.
NucleoSpin Kit Manual (MN) — Two modes: PCR cleanup or gel extraction. All spins at 11,000 × g.
PCR cleanup mode
  1. Add sample directly to column in collection tube; spin 30 s; discard flow-through.
  2. Wash: 700 µL NT3; spin 30 s; discard. Repeat once.
  3. Dry: spin 1 min (empty column).
  4. Elute: add 15–20 µL NE buffer; wait 1 min RT; transfer to fresh tube; spin 1 min.
Gel extraction mode
  1. Excise band under long-wave UV (365 nm) or blue-light transilluminator — minimise exposure time to reduce DNA damage.
  2. Weigh gel slice; add 200 µL NTI buffer per 100 mg gel; incubate 50°C until dissolved (5–10 min, vortex occasionally).
  3. Transfer to column; spin 30 s; discard flow-through.
  4. Wash: 700 µL NT3; spin 30 s; discard. Repeat once. Dry: spin 1 min.
  5. Elute: add 15–20 µL NE buffer (pre-warm to 70°C for fragments <500 bp); wait 1 min; spin 1 min.
Gel extraction yield is lower than PCR cleanup (~70–80%). Measure by NanoDrop after elution — do not assume concentration.

gRNA & Donor Design

ReferenceCRISPR
What is this? CRISPR-Cas9 edits genomic DNA by making a double-strand break at a locus defined by a guide RNA (gRNA). For knock-outs (KO), error-prone NHEJ repair introduces frameshift indels that disrupt the open reading frame. For knock-ins (KI), a short single-stranded donor (ssODN) is co-delivered so that HDR introduces a precise modification — a tag, point mutation, or short sequence. Careful gRNA and donor design is the most important determinant of success.
Before you start: write down your "source of truth" — which transcript/isoform you are targeting, the genomic coordinates of the edit site, and which protein terminus is being modified. This avoids ambiguity later when interpreting sequencing data or troubleshooting.
1 — Retrieve gene sequence (NCBI)
  1. Go to NCBI Gene and search for the gene of interest (e.g. "RBM39 human").
  2. Navigate to Genomic regions, transcripts, and products.
  3. Click GenBank (top right) → Send to → File. Download the RefSeq entry (NM_XXXXX).
  4. If unsure which is the canonical isoform, check UniProt for the canonical sequence annotation.
  5. Open the GenBank file in SnapGene: untick all features → scroll to the first (or full-length) protein → mark the CDS region.
2 — Select region of interest
  1. In SnapGene, navigate to your intended edit site (e.g. stop codon for C-terminal tag, start codon for N-terminal tag).
  2. Select a ~200 bp region centred on the edit site — this will be your gRNA design template and donor context.
  3. For N-terminal tags: target close to the start codon. For C-terminal tags: target just before the stop codon. Aim to cut as close to the insertion site as possible to maximise HDR efficiency.
3 — Design gRNA candidates (IDT)
  1. Go to IDT CRISPR Cas9 Design Tool — paste your ~200 bp region.
  2. IDT will return ranked gRNA candidates with on-target and off-target scores. Shortlist 2–3 candidates.
  3. (Knock-in only) for each candidate, verify in SnapGene that silencing the PAM site (to prevent re-cutting after HDR) does not change the amino acid sequence — use synonymous substitutions whenever possible.
  4. (Knock-out) target a constitutive exon shared across all relevant isoforms. Early exon frameshift indels are most reliable. Avoid exons subject to alternative splicing.
Always shortlist multiple gRNAs and test at least 2 in parallel. Off-target score alone does not predict editing efficiency — on-target score and proximity to edit site both matter.
Tip
Evaluating off-target sites — seed region rule: when reviewing off-target predictions from IDT or CRISPOR, pay attention to where the mismatches fall. Cas9 is highly intolerant to mismatches in the seed region (~10 nt immediately upstream of the PAM) — a single mismatch there is usually sufficient to abolish cleavage at that site. Mismatches in the PAM-distal end (positions 11–20 from PAM) are much better tolerated. So if you cannot find a gRNA with zero predicted off-targets: prioritise candidates where all predicted off-target sites carry ≥1 mismatch within positions 1–10 upstream of the PAM — those off-targets are effectively inert.
4 — (Knock-in) design the donor template (ssODN)
  1. Start from the WT genomic sequence around the cut site. In SnapGene, introduce your modification (tag sequence, point mutation, etc.).
  2. PAM silencing: mutate the PAM (NGG → NGA or similar) or the seed region of the protospacer (the ~12 bp proximal to PAM) to prevent Cas9 re-cutting the edited allele. Synonymous codon changes are preferred.
  3. Keep the donor ≤200 bp total. Place your modification as close to the cut site as possible — HDR efficiency drops with distance from the DSB.
  4. Typical design: ~50–80 bp homology arm — [tag/mutation] — ~50–80 bp homology arm.
  5. Order as a standard desalted ssODN from IDT (Ultramer if >60 nt, standard PAGE-purified for shorter).
5 — Pre-plan controls and record-keeping
  1. Plan controls before starting wet-lab work: untreated parental cells · mock electroporation (no RNP) · non-targeting guide.
  2. Record guide IDs, donor version IDs, transcript choice, and reagent lot numbers before starting.
  3. After the experiment: MiSeq amplicon sequencing is the definitive readout of editing outcome — plan your genotyping primers now (flanking the edit site, amplicon ~200–300 bp).
Tip
ssODN vs dsDNA donor: ssODNs (<200 nt) are efficient for small knock-ins (tags, point mutations) and are the standard choice for HiBiT or small epitope tag insertion. For larger inserts (>200 bp, e.g. full fluorescent protein), switch to a dsDNA donor (PCR product with homology arms or a plasmid). Asymmetric ssODN design (longer arm on the non-template strand relative to the nick) can improve HDR efficiency with some Cas9 variants.

CRISPR Knockout

CalculatorCRISPR
What is this? A CRISPR knockout uses Cas9 + a single gRNA to cut a target locus; error-prone NHEJ repair introduces frameshift indels that disrupt the reading frame — no donor template needed. The experiment is the same regardless of how Cas9/gRNA get into the cell; pick the delivery method below based on your cell line and what you have on hand.
Delivery method
PX458 (pSpCas9(BB)-2A-GFP, Addgene #48138) co-expresses SpCas9 and GFP from a single plasmid. The gRNA is cloned in via a ssOligo + NEB HiFi Assembly strategy. Cells are transfected and GFP-positive cells sorted by FACS.
Part 1 — Clone the gRNA into PX458
Step 1ssOligo design & order (IDT)
Order from IDT as Ultramer DNA Oligo (recommended for sequences >60 nt).
  1. Design your 20 bp gRNA spacer targeting the gene of interest. Verify PAM (NGG, immediately 3′ of spacer) and assess on-target/off-target scores using Benchling or CRISPOR.
  2. Design the ssOligo insert (≈ 70 nt total): 5′ homology arm (25 bp, matches PX458 upstream of the BbsI cut site) · 20 bp gRNA spacer · 3′ homology arm (25 bp, matches PX458 downstream of the BbsI cut site).
  3. Order at 100 nmol scale (standard desalt purification is sufficient).
  4. Upon receipt, resuspend in TE buffer + 50 mM NaCl to 100 µM stock; aliquot and freeze at −20°C.
  5. Prepare a 1 µM working stock: dilute 1 µL of 100 µM stock into 99 µL buffer.
Order the oligo first — you need it before you can assemble. Typical IDT turnaround is 1–3 business days for Ultramers.
Step 2Plasmid linearisation (BbsI digest)
  1. Digest 1 µg PX458 with BbsI (NEB) at 37°C for 30 min following the manufacturer's protocol — see calculator below.
  2. Confirm linearisation on a 1% agarose gel (expected band ≈ 9.3 kb).
  3. Excise the band and purify the linearised plasmid (NucleoSpin or equivalent). Measure concentration by NanoDrop — needed for the calculator below.
Step 3HiFi assembly & transformation
NEBuilder HiFi DNA Assembly Master Mix (NEB E2621)
  1. Set up the reaction on ice using the volumes from the calculator. Add components in order: water → vector → ssOligo → master mix.
  2. Incubate at 50°C for 60 min in a thermocycler (lid off or set to 55°C).
  3. Transform 2 µL into NEB Stable Competent E. coli (C3040H) — see the Transformation protocol; heat shock 42°C × 30 s.
  4. Plate on LB+ampicillin (100 µg/mL); pick 6–8 colonies; miniprep and verify by Sanger sequencing across the cloning site.
Part 2 — Transfect & enrich
Day 0 — Cell seeding
  1. Plate 1 × 10⁶ wild-type cells per well in a 6-well plate.
  2. Always include a negative control well (no transfection) seeded in parallel — used to set the GFP gate at FACS and confirm baseline.
Day 1 — Transfection (FugeneHD, 6-well plate)
  1. Tube A — add 1 µg PX458-gRNA plasmid to 100 µL Opti-MEM. Mix gently.
  2. Tube B — add 3 µL FugeneHD (Promega) to 100 µL Opti-MEM. Mix gently.
  3. Combine Tube A + Tube B, mix gently, incubate 5 min at RT.
  4. Add the entire complex dropwise to the well (no media change needed).
  5. Incubate cells with the transfection reagent for 8–24 h, then sort GFP-positive cells by FACS to enrich for Cas9-expressing transfected cells → proceed to Single Cell Sorting for clonal expansion.
PX458 expresses GFP as a transfection marker — GFP+ cells are Cas9-expressing but not necessarily edited. Sort into wells for clonal expansion, then genotype by Sanger/ICE analysis to confirm KO.
Electroporation delivers the CRISPR RNP (Cas9 protein pre-complexed with the annealed gRNA duplex) directly into cells using a brief electrical pulse. Avoids DNA integration of Cas9, reduces off-target activity, and gives transient expression that disappears within days. No cloning needed — faster turnaround than PX458.
Reconstitution — TracrRNA & crRNA
  1. tracrRNA: 20 nmol → 200 µL Nuclease-Free duplex buffer (IDT) → 100 µM stock.
  2. crRNA: 2 nmol → 20 µL duplex buffer → 100 µM stock.
  3. Spin down after reconstitution.
Reconstitution — Cas9
  1. Add 50 µL reconstitution solution to lyophilized S. pyogenes Cas9 → 1 µg/µL.
  2. Vortex gently, spin down, incubate 10 min on ice or overnight in fridge.
Anneal TracrRNA + crRNA
  1. Add 5 µL of each (TracrRNA + crRNA) per tube (1 tube per crRNA).
  2. Incubate at 95°C × 5 min, then spin down.
RNP complex formation (per 2 electroporations, ~0.4M cells)
12 µL R buffer + 1 µL gRNA + 1.2 µL Cas9
  1. Mix both components and incubate 20 min at RT.
Cell preparation
  1. Trypsinize, neutralize, wash with PBS, resuspend in 1 mL PBS.
  2. Count: add 2×10⁵ cells/electroporation to Eppendorf.
  3. Remove PBS by centrifugation.
  4. Add 2 mL media to 6-well plate; seed same number of cells in negative control well.
Electroporation
  1. Fill 50 mL falcon with ethanol, insert tips; aspirate and dry on paper; wash with PBS.
  2. Add RNP mix to cell pellet; resuspend gently without generating bubbles.
  3. Add 3 mL electrolyte solution to electroporation vessel.
  4. Take 10 µL of mix with electroporating pipette → place in vessel → electroporate → dispense into well. Repeat per well.
  5. Leave cells 48–72 h before analysis/sorting.
After electroporation — pool validation (before single-cell sorting)
Why validate the pool first? Single-cell sorting is time-consuming. Confirming editing in the pool first avoids committing to clonal expansion if the edit rate is too low.
  1. Maintain edited cells as a pooled population for 48–72 h minimum before analysis (allow NHEJ to resolve).
  2. Check by MiSeq amplicon sequencing around the cut site — aim for >70% frameshift indels before sorting. Optionally confirm loss of protein by western blot.
  3. Define a go/no-go criterion before sorting (e.g. >70% indels by MiSeq or a clean TIDE trace). If below threshold, consider re-electroporating with a different guide.
Tip
TIDE analysis (tide.nki.nl) on Sanger sequencing of the pool gives a fast estimate of editing efficiency and indel spectrum before committing to single-cell sorting.
Tip
When to use which method: PX458 suits adherent lines that tolerate lipofection, with GFP⁺ enrichment as a bonus — but longer Cas9 expression means slightly higher off-target risk and there's cloning to do first. RNP electroporation is faster (no cloning), lower off-target exposure (transient Cas9), and works on hard-to-transfect lines and primary cells — but needs an electroporator.

CRISPR Knock-in

CalculatorCRISPR
What is this? A CRISPR knock-in co-delivers a donor template with Cas9/gRNA so that HDR incorporates a precise modification — a tag, point mutation, or short sequence — at the cut site. The experiment is the same regardless of how the components get into the cell; pick the delivery method below based on insert size and cell line.
Delivery method
Strategy: PX458 (pSpCas9(BB)-2A-GFP, Addgene #48138) co-expresses Cas9 and GFP. Co-transfect with a plasmid donor bearing the insert flanked by homology arms (HAs). GFP⁺ cells sorted by FACS are enriched for Cas9-expressing (and therefore editing-competent) cells. Best for large inserts >200 bp, fluorescent tags, or selection cassettes.
1 — Clone gRNA into PX458
Step 1Design & order ssOligo insert
Order from IDT as Ultramer DNA Oligo (recommended for sequences >60 nt).
  1. Design your 20 bp gRNA spacer. Verify PAM (NGG) and assess scores using Benchling or CRISPOR.
  2. Design an ssOligo with: 5′ homology arm (25 bp) matching PX458 upstream of BbsI cut · gRNA spacer (20 bp) · 3′ homology arm (25 bp) matching PX458 downstream of BbsI cut.
  3. Dissolve lyophilised ssOligo in nuclease-free water to 1 µM working stock.
Step 2Plasmid linearisation (BbsI digest)
  1. Digest 1 µg PX458 with BbsI (NEB) at 37°C for 30 min following the manufacturer's protocol — see calculator below.
  2. Confirm linearisation on a 1% agarose gel (expected band ≈ 9.3 kb).
  3. Excise the band and purify the linearised plasmid (NucleoSpin or equivalent). Measure concentration by NanoDrop — needed for the calculator below.
Step 3HiFi assembly & transformation
NEBuilder HiFi DNA Assembly Master Mix (NEB E2621)
  1. Set up the reaction on ice using the volumes from the calculator. Add components in order: water → vector → ssOligo → master mix.
  2. Incubate at 50°C for 60 min in a thermocycler (lid off or 55°C).
  3. Transform 2 µL into NEB Stable E. coli (C3040H). Plate on LB + ampicillin (100 µg/mL) overnight 37°C.
  4. Pick 6–8 colonies; miniprep; verify by Sanger sequencing across the cloning junction.
2 — Plasmid donor design
  1. Clone: Left HA (400–800 bp) — Insert — Right HA (400–800 bp) into a donor backbone. HAs must be perfectly homologous to the genomic locus.
  2. Silence the PAM in the donor (1–2 silent mutations in the seed region) to prevent Cas9 from re-cutting after HDR.
  3. Add a silent diagnostic SNP ~10–15 bp from the cut site to distinguish the HDR allele from WT by sequencing.
  4. Zhang 2017 double-cut: design a second sgRNA on the opposite side of the insert; the two cuts release the insert+HAs from the circular plasmid. Use the same PX458 backbone or a separate gRNA plasmid.
  5. Maxiprep both PX458 and donor (endotoxin-free). A260/280 ≥ 1.8; A260/230 ≥ 2.0.
3 — Transfection (Lipofectamine 3000)
  1. Seed cells at 60–70% confluency the day before in a 6-well plate or 10 cm dish.
  2. Transfect: PX458 1.5 µg + plasmid donor 3–4 µg per well (ratio ~1:2–3 Cas9:donor). Use Lipofectamine 3000 per manufacturer protocol in Opti-MEM.
  3. Incubate 24–48 h. Check GFP by fluorescence microscopy — expect 20–60% GFP⁺ in transfectable lines.
  4. Do not select with antibiotics during the first 48 h post-transfection.
4 — FACS sorting & clonal expansion
  1. 48 h post-transfection: trypsinise, resuspend in sorting buffer (PBS + 2% FBS + 10 mM HEPES, filter 0.22 µm).
  2. Sort GFP⁺ cells (488 nm excitation). Gate top 10–20% GFP⁺ for enrichment of Cas9-expressing cells.
  3. Single-cell deposit into 96-well plates — see Single Cell Sorting protocol.
5 — Clone screening
  1. After 2–3 weeks expand colonies. Screen by junction PCR: one primer outside the HA, one inside the insert. A band indicates donor integration.
  2. Sanger-sequence PCR product across both junctions (5′ and 3′) and the full insert.
  3. Assess zygosity: run both junction PCRs + a WT-allele PCR. Heterozygous clones are common — confirm homozygosity by allele-specific sequencing or digital PCR.
  4. Validate protein by Western blot, co-IP, or immunofluorescence before functional use.
Strategy: pre-assembled Cas9 RNP (Cas9 protein + annealed crRNA:tracrRNA) is electroporated together with a single-stranded oligodeoxynucleotide (ssODN) donor. HDR incorporates the ssODN precisely at the cut site. Ideal for small edits: point mutations, epitope tags (HA, FLAG, V5), short linkers, or loxP sites (<200 bp total insert). No plasmid cloning needed — fast turnaround. Works best with Neon (Thermo) or 4D-Nucleofector (Lonza).
1 — ssODN design
  1. Total length 100–200 nt. Flanking HAs of 40–80 nt each side of the edit. Asymmetric HAs (longer on the PAM-distal side) can improve HDR frequency.
  2. Silence the PAM in the ssODN to prevent re-cutting. Introduce a silent diagnostic SNP ~10 bp from the cut site.
  3. Order on the non-template strand (strand not used by Cas9 as template for nicking) — often gives higher HDR.
  4. Standard desalted synthesis sufficient for ≤120 nt; PAGE-purify if >120 nt. Resuspend in TE pH 8.0 at 100 µM.
2 — RNP assembly (IDT Alt-R system)
  1. Resuspend crRNA and tracrRNA to 200 µM in nuclease-free IDT Duplex Buffer.
  2. Mix equimolar crRNA + tracrRNA; anneal at 95°C for 5 min, then cool slowly to RT in a heat block (turn off heater). Final gRNA duplex: 100 µM.
  3. Mix gRNA duplex + Cas9 protein at 1:1 molar ratio (e.g. 1 µL 61 µM Cas9 + 1 µL 100 µM gRNA → dilute to working [RNP] ~3 µM in PBS). Incubate 10–15 min at RT.
  4. Add ssODN separately — do not pre-mix with RNP more than a few seconds before electroporation.
3 — Electroporation
  1. Harvest cells in log phase (70–80% confluency). Viability must be >90% before electroporation.
  2. Pellet 1–5 × 10⁵ cells at 300 × g for 5 min. Wash once with PBS (no Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺). Remove all residual PBS.
  3. Resuspend in electroporation buffer: Neon: Buffer R (10 µL tip) or Buffer T; 4D: SF or SE buffer (20 µL) per cell-line optimisation guide.
  4. Add RNP (1–2 µL, ~100–200 pmol Cas9) + ssODN (1–2 µL, 100–200 pmol) to the cell suspension. Mix gently — do not vortex.
  5. Neon parameters (HEK293T/HCT116): 1400–1500 V · 20 ms · 1 pulse. Optimise per cell type.
    4D-Nucleofector: use manufacturer pre-set code for your cell line (e.g. CM-130 for HEK293).
  6. Transfer immediately to pre-warmed antibiotic-free medium. Do not disturb for 10 min.
4 — Recovery & clonal expansion
  1. 24 h post-electroporation: check viability and refresh medium. Expect 50–80% viability.
  2. 48–72 h: harvest a small aliquot for gDNA extraction. Run ICE analysis (Synthego) or T7E1 to confirm RNP activity — expect 20–60% indels as proxy for cutting efficiency.
  3. Single-cell sort or serially dilute into 96-well plates for clonal expansion — see Single Cell Sorting protocol.
5 — Genotyping HDR clones
  1. Design PCR primers outside the ssODN HA region to avoid amplifying residual ssODN template. Expected amplicon 300–600 bp.
  2. Run PCR product on gel to detect size difference (if insert ≥20 bp). Sequence all clones regardless.
  3. Sanger-sequence to confirm the exact edit. Check both alleles — heterozygous clones are common with ssODN HDR.
  4. Validate protein expression and function by Western blot and/or co-IP before use in experiments.
Tip
When to use which method: PX458 + plasmid donor suits larger inserts (HiBiT-mNG fusions, selection cassettes, full FPs) and adherent lines that tolerate lipofection, with GFP⁺ enrichment as a bonus. Longer Cas9 expression → slightly higher off-target risk; always validate by junction PCR. For small edits (tags ≤200 nt, point mutations), hard-to-transfect lines, or primary cells, RNP + ssODN electroporation is faster with no cloning and lower off-target exposure.

What is this? Immunoprecipitation (IP) uses a specific antibody to pull down a target protein — along with any interacting partners — from a cell lysate.   2. Incubate 10 min in fridge; redistribute once.

DIA-NN — Data-Independent Acquisition Analysis

ReferenceProteomics
What is DIA-NN? DIA-NN is a software pipeline for processing data-independent acquisition (DIA) mass spectrometry data. Unlike DDA (data-dependent acquisition), DIA fragments all ions in a scan window simultaneously — this gives better quantitative reproducibility across samples. DIA-NN deconvolutes the resulting complex spectra by matching against in silico-predicted spectral libraries generated from a FASTA file. The output is a protein group quantification matrix ready for statistical analysis.
DIA-NN GitHub (Demichev lab) · v1.8 and v1.9 supported
Maintain strict sample order, labelling, and classification before you start — mislabelled samples will produce wrong differential comparisons downstream and are very hard to catch after the fact.
A — Data organisation
  1. Create a new folder inside the RAW data directory for this experiment (e.g. Samples_1-20_COMPOUND_DATE).
  2. All DIA-NN outputs and downstream analyses must live in this folder.
  3. Write down your sample order before opening DIA-NN — you will need it for Perseus annotation.
B — FASTA file preparation
  1. Use the human whole-proteome FASTA available on the computer room workstation (contaminants already added).
  2. If you have custom sequences to detect (knock-in tags, HiBiT, fluorescent proteins, exogenous constructs): open the FASTA in a text editor and add them manually in standard FASTA format:
    >sp|CUSTOM|MY_TAG_HUMAN
    MGSSHHHHHH...
  3. Why this matters: DIA-NN generates in silico tryptic peptides from this file and matches experimental MS2 spectra against them. Any protein not in the FASTA will not be quantified, regardless of how many peptides you have for it.
C — DIA-NN processing setup
  1. Load raw files: top left panel → RAW → Select all → Open (all .raw or .mzML files for this experiment).
  2. Add FASTA: click "Add FASTA" → select your prepared FASTA file.
  3. Library generation: enable "FASTA digest for library-free search" — generates the spectral library on the fly from your FASTA. Enable deep learning-based spectra and RT prediction.
  4. General settings: leave all defaults unchanged.
  5. MBR (Match Between Runs): ✅ ENABLE — uses retention time alignment to transfer identifications between runs, dramatically improving quantitative completeness, especially for low-abundance proteins.
  6. Missed cleavages: set to 1 (allows one missed trypsin cleavage site per peptide — increases sensitivity without too much noise).
  7. Output folder: set to the folder created in step A.
  8. Click Run — runtime is ~4+ h for a full dataset.
D — Key output files
  1. report.pg_matrix.tsv — main output: protein group quantification matrix. This is what you load into Perseus.
  2. report.tsv — precursor-level report, more granular, useful for troubleshooting.
  3. report.stats.tsv — per-run QC stats: number of proteins identified, median CV, etc. Check this first to spot problematic runs before statistical analysis.
Tip
v1.9 contaminant handling: DIA-NN 1.9 allows adding contaminants as a separate dedicated FASTA rather than embedding them in the main FASTA. If using this approach, remove the contaminant filtering step from Perseus (Perseus step D) — they will already be excluded at the DIA-NN level.

Perseus — Statistical Analysis of Proteomics Data

ReferenceProteomics
What is Perseus? Perseus (Max Planck Institute) is the standard tool for statistical analysis of quantitative proteomics data. It takes the protein quantification matrix from DIA-NN and applies log transformation, filtering, imputation of missing values, and statistical tests (volcano plots, t-tests). The workflow below goes step by step — order matters.
Perseus download and documentation — save your session frequently (Perseus crashes without warning).
A — Import data
  1. Top left → Generic matrix upload.
  2. Select report.pg_matrix.tsv from your DIA-NN output folder.
  3. Text format is auto-detected — confirm.
  4. Select all sample columns → assign to "Main" (these are your quantitative intensity values).
  5. Click OK → Save As immediately (File → Save As) with a descriptive name. Do this before any processing.
B — Sample annotation
  1. Go to Annotation rows (top menu).
  2. Add categorical annotation → assign group labels per sample column (e.g. Samples 1–4 → "Compound_1", Samples 5–8 → "DMSO").
  3. Why this matters: Perseus uses these group labels to define comparisons in the volcano plot and t-test. Mislabelling here means wrong statistics — double-check against your sample list from DIA-NN.
  4. Tick the checkbox → OK.
C — Log₂ transformation
  1. Top left → Basic → apply log₂ transform to all Main columns.
  2. Why: raw intensity values span many orders of magnitude and are not normally distributed. Log₂ transformation compresses the range, making distributions more symmetric — required for t-tests and PCA to be valid. A 1-unit change in log₂ = 2-fold change in abundance.
D — Filter contaminants and low-quality IDs
Why filter before imputing? Imputation fills in missing values by sampling from a noise distribution. If you impute before filtering, you introduce noise-based values for proteins that were never real identifications. Filter first, then impute.
  1. Processing → Filter rows → Filter rows based on categorical column.
  2. Remove Reverse (+) — decoy/reverse-sequence hits, not real protein IDs.
  3. Remove Only identified by site (+) — lower-confidence identifications supported only by modified peptides.
  4. Remove Contaminants (+) — common sample prep contaminants (keratins, trypsin, albumin…).
  5. Note: if loading DIA-NN output directly, these columns may not exist — skip this step if absent.
E — Filter by valid values
  1. Processing → Filter rows → Filter rows based on valid values.
  2. Recommended setting: minimum 2 valid values in at least one group — keeps proteins quantified in at least one condition, even if missing in the other.
  3. Avoid requiring valid values in all groups — this discards many real hits genuinely absent in one condition (e.g. degraded proteins).
  4. Optional: additionally filter by Peptides > 2.
F — Missing value imputation
  1. Top left → Imputation → replace missing values from normal distribution.
  2. Leave default settings (downshift = 1.8, width = 0.3) → OK.
  3. Why impute? Statistical tests require complete matrices. Proteins missing in one condition but quantified in another are real biological signals — imputing from the low end of the distribution allows these to appear as significant hits in the volcano plot.
G — Exploratory analysis: PCA (optional but recommended)
  1. Top middle → Principal Component Analysis.
  2. Replicates should cluster tightly together; different conditions should separate.
  3. An outlier replicate that clusters away from its group signals a problem (failed sample prep, labelling error, degraded sample) — consider excluding it and re-running.
H — Differential analysis: volcano plot
  1. Top middle → Volcano Plot.
  2. Define comparison: e.g. "Compound_1" vs "DMSO" using your categorical annotation.
  3. Settings: t-test type = Student's t-test; FDR = 0.05; S0 = 0.1 (controls curve shape — increase to be more stringent).
  4. Proteins in the upper right (enriched) or upper left (depleted/degraded) above the significance curve are your hits.
I — Export results
  1. Right-click any protein in the volcano → Export → Plain matrix.
  2. Save as .txt; open in Excel for further annotation.
  3. Also export the full processed matrix: File → Export → Generic matrix export → .txt.
Tip
For degradation proteomics, you typically care about proteins specifically depleted (left side of volcano) with statistical significance. A clean degrader will show your POI as the most significantly depleted protein with the largest fold change. Co-depleted proteins can indicate on-target collateral effects or off-target degradation — worth following up. The S0 parameter controls the minimum fold change required for significance.

Finding & Evaluating Crystal Structures (PDB)

ReferenceStructural Biology
Why structure matters in chemical biology: before designing a PROTAC, inhibitor, or degrader, you want to understand the 3D shape of your target — where ligands bind, which residues are important, whether a surface is druggable, and how two proteins might interact. Structural data is your map. Even an imperfect map (AlphaFold) is far better than no map at all.
What is the PDB? The Protein Data Bank (PDB) is a public archive of experimentally determined 3D structures of proteins, nucleic acids, and complexes. Each entry is identified by a 4-character code (e.g. 6HAX). Structures are determined by X-ray crystallography, cryo-EM, or NMR. Not all structures are equal — resolution, completeness, and experimental conditions matter.
1 — Search for your protein
  1. Go to rcsb.org — search by protein name, gene name, UniProt ID, or ligand (e.g. "CRBN", "VHL", "BRD4").
  2. Filter results by: Organism (prefer Homo sapiens) · Experimental method (X-ray or cryo-EM) · Resolution (lower Å = better; aim for ≤2.5 Å for atomic detail).
  3. Sort by resolution — the best structure is usually at the top, but check it has a ligand if you want to see the binding pocket.
2 — Evaluate a structure before using it
Why evaluate? A structure in the PDB is not automatically "good". Check these before building conclusions on it.
  1. Resolution: the number in Å. <2.0 Å = excellent; 2.0–2.5 Å = good; 2.5–3.5 Å = usable but be careful; >3.5 Å = low confidence on side-chain positions.
  2. R-factor / R-free: shown on the PDB page under "Refinement". R-free <0.25 is acceptable; big difference between R and R-free suggests overfitting.
  3. Completeness: check if your region of interest has missing residues (shown as gaps in the sequence viewer) — disordered loops are often absent.
  4. Ligand: if the structure has your ligand of interest, check the electron density around it — click "3D View" and look for the green mesh around the ligand. No mesh = no confidence.
  5. Biological assembly: some proteins are dimers or tetramers — check the "Biological Assembly" tab, not just the asymmetric unit.
3 — Download the structure
  1. Click Download Files (top right of any PDB entry) → choose PDB Format (.pdb) for PyMOL, or mmCIF for ChimeraX/Coot.
  2. For large structures or cryo-EM maps, the mmCIF format is preferred.
  3. Save with a meaningful name: e.g. CRBN_lenalidomide_6HAX.pdb.
4 — Useful PDB tools
  1. RCSB Pairwise Structure Alignment — compare two structures online without PyMOL.
  2. PDBe-KB — aggregates all structures for a given UniProt entry; shows coverage across the sequence.
  3. UniProt → Structure tab — links directly to all PDB entries for your protein, with sequence coverage shown visually.
Tip
For PROTAC/degrader design, you ideally want structures of both the target protein (POI) and the E3 ligase (e.g. CRBN or VHL) with their respective ligands bound. This lets you think about where the linker exits each binding pocket and which solvent-exposed vectors are available for elaboration. The RCSB Advanced Search lets you filter by "Has Ligand" to find co-crystal structures directly.

AlphaFold — Structure Prediction

ReferenceStructural Biology
What is AlphaFold? AlphaFold2 (DeepMind, 2021) predicts protein 3D structure from amino acid sequence alone with remarkable accuracy. It doesn't tell you about ligand binding or dynamics, but it gives you a starting model when no crystal structure exists. The EBI hosts pre-computed models for essentially the entire human proteome — free to download in seconds.
When to use AlphaFold vs a crystal structure
Always prefer an experimental structure (PDB) over AlphaFold when one exists at good resolution, especially around the binding site. Crystal structures capture real ligand-induced conformational changes; AlphaFold does not model bound ligands and may miss binding-relevant conformations.
  1. Use AlphaFold when: no PDB structure exists for your protein · you need a model of a domain not covered by any crystal structure · you want to predict the fold of a mutant or tagged construct.
  2. Use a crystal structure when: you want to understand ligand binding · you need accurate side-chain positions in the binding pocket · you're doing docking.
1 — AlphaFold database (pre-computed models)
  1. Go to alphafold.ebi.ac.uk.
  2. Search by protein name or UniProt ID (e.g. "DCAF15" or "Q9NWF9").
  3. Download the .pdb file from the page — this is your AlphaFold model.
  4. The page also shows the pLDDT score mapped onto the structure — read this before trusting any region (see below).
2 — Understanding pLDDT (confidence score)
pLDDT (predicted Local Distance Difference Test) runs from 0–100 and is colour-coded on the model:
🔵 Dark blue (>90): very high confidence — trust this backbone and most side chains
🟦 Light blue (70–90): good confidence — backbone reliable, some side-chain uncertainty
🟡 Yellow (50–70): low confidence — backbone rough, side chains unreliable. Often intrinsically disordered regions.
🟠 Orange (<50): very low confidence — likely disordered or a flexible loop. Do not interpret these regions structurally.
3 — AlphaFold3 / ColabFold for custom predictions
  1. AlphaFold Server (AF3) — predict structures of proteins, protein–protein complexes, or protein–small molecule complexes. Free, no installation. Log in with Google. Submit sequence(s) and wait ~10–30 min.
  2. ColabFold (Google Colab) — faster, also free, good for multimers. Useful when AF3 server queue is long.
  3. For a ternary complex (POI + E3 ligase), you can submit both sequences to AF3 as a multimer — the PAE plot tells you how confident the interface prediction is.
4 — PAE plot (for multimers)
PAE (Predicted Aligned Error) is shown as a 2D matrix when you predict a complex. Low PAE between two chains (dark green squares off the diagonal) = AlphaFold is confident about the relative positioning of those two chains = credible interface prediction. High PAE = uncertain relative positioning — treat the interface with caution.
Tip
AlphaFold3 can now include small molecule ligands (cofactors, metals) in predictions. For PROTAC design, you can submit POI + CRBN/VHL + a SMILES string of your compound to get a rough ternary complex model. These are speculative but useful for early-stage linker vector thinking. Don't over-interpret side-chain positions in the predicted interface.

Binding Site & Pocket Analysis

ReferenceStructural Biology
Why identify pockets? To design a ligand, you need to know where to put it. If a co-crystal structure with a known ligand exists, the pocket is obvious — just look at where the ligand sits. But for apo structures (no ligand) or novel proteins, you need computational tools to identify cavities large enough and shaped correctly to bind a drug-like molecule.
Case 1 — Structure has a known ligand (easiest)
  1. Load structure in PyMOL, zoom to ligand: zoom organic.
  2. The cavity around the ligand is your binding pocket. Select residues within 4–5 Å: select pocket, byres (organic expand 4).
  3. Show surface of pocket to see its shape: show surface, pocket.
  4. Identify hydrophobic vs polar residues — relevant for ligand design.
Case 2 — Apo structure or no ligand (use CASTp)
  1. Go to CASTp server — upload your .pdb file.
  2. Set probe radius to 1.4 Å (standard, size of a water molecule).
  3. Submit — results come back within minutes as a list of pockets ranked by volume (Å³).
  4. Download the annotated PDB and load in PyMOL to visualise which pocket is which.
  5. Larger volume + concave shape + hydrophobic character = more druggable pocket.
Druggability assessment
Not all pockets are druggable. A pocket needs to be: (1) large enough (~300–1000 Å³ for a drug-sized molecule), (2) sufficiently enclosed / concave (not a flat surface), (3) has a mix of hydrophobic and H-bonding residues to engage a ligand. Protein–protein interaction (PPI) interfaces are notoriously hard — flat and large. PPI inhibitors (like BET bromodomain inhibitors) work because the interface has a hot-spot cavity.
Comparing pockets between structures (conformational changes)
  1. Load apo and holo (ligand-bound) structures; align them: align structure_apo, structure_holo.
  2. Look for induced-fit changes: loop movements, helix shifts around the binding site.
  3. This is relevant for understanding whether your target has an induced-fit mechanism (relevant for fragment-based design).
Tip
For PROTAC targets, an interesting question is not just "where does my POI ligand bind?" but also "is there a surface patch on my POI that is close to the E3 ligase surface in a productive ternary complex?" — the neo-substrate interface concept. Structures of CRBN or VHL with their known degraders give you a template for where the linker exits — use these as a guide when thinking about linker vectors on your own target.

Ternary Complex Modelling (PROTAC / Degrader)

ReferenceStructural Biology
What is a ternary complex? A PROTAC simultaneously binds both your target protein (POI) and an E3 ubiquitin ligase (e.g. CRBN or VHL), bringing them into proximity so the E3 can ubiquitinate the POI. The ternary complex (POI–PROTAC–E3) is the active species for degradation. Understanding its geometry helps explain why some PROTACs work and others don't, and guides linker design.
Why model it?
  1. Ternary complex formation is cooperative — a good PROTAC stabilises a specific POI:E3 interface beyond what the two proteins alone would form.
  2. The geometry of the complex (which POI surface faces the E3, distance to ubiquitination site) determines degradation efficiency.
  3. Models help you identify: which linker exit vectors are productive · whether linker length is appropriate · whether there are clashes at the interface.
Step 1 — Gather your starting structures
  1. POI with its ligand bound (from PDB or docked).
  2. E3 ligase with its ligand bound — for CRBN: PDB 4CI3 (thalidomide) or search for your specific warhead; for VHL: PDB 4B9K (VHL-HIF peptide).
  3. If no co-crystal: dock your ligands first using Glide (Schrödinger) or Gnina (free).
Step 2 — Predict the ternary complex
  1. AlphaFold3 (easiest, free): go to alphafoldserver.com → add sequences for both POI and E3 ligase → optionally add your PROTAC SMILES as a ligand → Submit. Download the .cif result.
  2. HDOCK (protein–protein docking, free): go to hdock.phys.hust.edu.cn → upload both proteins → optionally restrain interface residues if you have HDX or mutagenesis data → Submit. Download top models.
  3. ClusPro (alternative, free): cluspro.bu.edu — similar workflow, sometimes better for large complexes.
Step 3 — Evaluate the model in PyMOL
  1. Load the complex: drag .pdb/.cif onto PyMOL.
  2. Colour each chain differently: color slate, chain A · color salmon, chain B.
  3. Find the ligands: show sticks, organic · color yellow, organic.
  4. Measure distance between the two ligand exit vectors (where the linker would attach): distance linker_dist, resi X and name CX, resi Y and name CY.
  5. Check for clashes at the interface: select interface, byres (chain A within 5 of chain B) then show sticks, interface.
  6. Check that lysines on the POI surface near the E3 are accessible: Lys residues within ubiquitination range (~40–50 Å from E2 active site Cys) are productive.
Step 4 — Linker length estimation
A rough rule: each PEG unit (~3.8 Å) or each carbon in an alkyl chain (~1.25 Å extended) contributes to the linker length. Measure the distance between the two exit vectors in your model and use this to estimate minimum linker length needed. In practice, linkers 4–14 atoms long are most common in active PROTACs, but the geometry of your specific complex matters more than a rule of thumb.
Tip
The Ciulli lab has published extensively on ternary complex structures and cooperativity. Notable resources: the 2019 Nature Chemical Biology paper on PROTAC cooperativity and PDB 6BN7 (SMARCA2 BRD–ACBI1–VHL ternary complex crystal structure) are excellent reference points for what a productive ternary complex looks like structurally. Worth loading 6BN7 in PyMOL as a learning exercise before tackling your own target.

TR-FRET — Ternary Complex Formation Assay

ReferenceBiophysics
What is this? Time-Resolved FRET (TR-FRET) uses a long-lifetime lanthanide donor (typically Terbium, Tb) and a short-lifetime acceptor (e.g. Alexa Fluor 488, d2, or BODIPY). By gating the measurement after a delay (>50 µs), background autofluorescence is eliminated. FRET only occurs when donor and acceptor are within ~10 nm, i.e. when a ternary complex (POI–PROTAC–E3 ligase) forms. In the PROTAC context, TR-FRET is the gold-standard biochemical assay to detect and quantify ternary complex formation, determine the maximal efficacy concentration (Cmax), and characterise cooperativity (α) — all in a homogeneous, no-wash format suitable for HTS in 384-well plates.
When to use it: early-to-mid PROTAC campaign — comparing a series of linker lengths or exit vectors, determining the bell-shaped dose-response and hook effect, measuring cooperativity vs. FP/SPR. Requires purified recombinant proteins with orthogonal tags (e.g. GST on POI, His on CRBN-DDB1). CoraFluor-1 (Tb cryptate) anti-His nanobody plus GST-antibody labelled with Lumi4-Tb is the Ciulli lab preferred reagent set (Payne & Mazitschek 2022).
Principle & key parameters
  1. Donor: Tb-labelled antibody or nanobody against tag on E3 ligase subunit (e.g. Tb-anti-His against His₆-CRBN-DDB1). Emission measured at 490 nm (Tb) and 520 nm (acceptor). Ratio = 10,000 × (520 nm / 490 nm) = ratiometric TR-FRET unit (RTU).
  2. Acceptor: fluorophore-labelled antibody against tag on POI (e.g. AF488-anti-GST against GST-BRD4). Must have good spectral overlap with Tb emission.
  3. Bell-shaped curve: at low [PROTAC], ternary complex increases (rising phase); at high [PROTAC], binary complexes dominate and displace ternary (hook effect / falling phase). The peak defines Cmax and EC50,ternary.
  4. Cooperativity α: α = KD,binary / KD,ternary. α > 1 = positive cooperativity. Determine by comparing FP IC50 with and without saturating POI.
  5. DMSO tolerance: typically ≤1% DMSO final. Test DMSO titration before full compound screen — TR-FRET signal should be flat up to 1% and drop only above 2%.
Reagents & setup
  1. Buffer: 25 mM HEPES pH 7.4, 150 mM NaCl, 0.5 mM TCEP, 0.05% Tween-20, 0.1% BSA. Prepare fresh; Tween-20 and BSA critical to prevent aggregation and non-specific binding.
  2. Purified proteins: E3 ligase with orthogonal tag (e.g. His₆-CRBN-DDB1), POI with orthogonal tag (e.g. GST-BRD4(BD1)). Check purity ≥90% by SDS-PAGE/Coomassie. Flash-freeze aliquots; avoid repeated freeze-thaw.
  3. Detection reagents: Tb-labelled antibody/nanobody against E3 tag (donor); fluorophore-labelled antibody against POI tag (acceptor). Optimise concentration via matrix titration (see below).
  4. Plates: white 384-well low-volume plates (e.g. Greiner 784075). White walls maximise signal. 10–20 µL total reaction volume.
  5. Compound: prepare 10–11 point dose-response (typically 10 µM – 0.1 nM, 3-fold dilutions) in 100% DMSO, then dilute 1:100 into assay to achieve 1% DMSO final. Include DMSO control and positive control (known PROTAC at Cmax).
Optimisation (stepwise, after Sakamoto et al. ACS PTSI 2021)
  1. Step 1 — Antibody titration: fix POI and E3 at low nM; titrate donor Ab (0.25–8 nM) and acceptor Ab (0.5–16 nM) in a matrix. Aim: Tb-anti-His ~2 nM, AF488-anti-GST ~2–4 nM.
  2. Step 2 — Protein titration: fix optimal antibody concentrations. Titrate POI and E3 (1–20 nM each). For CRBN-based PROTACs: GST-BRD4 ~2 nM, His-CRBN-DDB1 ~8 nM (4:1 ratio) is a well-validated starting point.
  3. Step 3 — Incubation time: measure signal at 30, 60, 120, 180, 300 min. Typically 120–180 min at RT in the dark is sufficient for most PROTACs.
  4. Step 4 — DMSO tolerance: add 0, 0.5, 1, 2, 4% DMSO. Assay should be stable at ≤1% DMSO.
  5. Step 5 — PROTAC dose-response: run full 10-point curve under optimised conditions. Fit to bell-shaped model or report EC50 and EC50,hook separately.
Protocol — 384-well setup (20 uL)
Reagent system: Alexa Fluor 647-labelled Protein A (donor on POI), biotinylated Protein B (E3 ligase subunit), LANCE Streptavidin-Europium (PerkinElmer #AD0062) as the lanthanide donor bridged via biotin. This LANCE-based format uses Eu chelate (excitation 337 nm) with AF647 as acceptor (emission 665 nm), and a short-wavelength reference channel at 620 nm for ratiometric correction.
  1. Prepare assay buffer: 25 mM HEPES pH 7.5, 50 mM NaCl, 2 mM TCEP, 0.005% Tween-20. Prepare fresh; TCEP critical for cysteine-containing proteins.
  2. Prepare assay solution with final concentrations: Alexa647-labelled Protein A 25 nM, biotinylated Protein B 2.5 nM, LANCE Streptavidin Fluorophore 0.25 nM. Mix in this order; allow Streptavidin-biotin interaction to form before adding compound.
  3. Dispense compounds into Corning 384-well black plates. Echo Acoustic Dispenser recommended for DMSO compound stocks.
  4. Add assay solution: 20 µL/well final volume. Seal plate with foil.
  5. Incubate at room temperature for 17 hours (overnight) — extended incubation ensures equilibrium for slow-forming ternary complexes.
  6. Read on PHERAstar FS (BMG LABTECH): excitation 337 nm, dual emission 620 nm (Eu reference) and 665 nm (AF647 FRET signal). Integration time 70–400 µs.
  7. Calculate TR-FRET ratio = 665 nm / 620 nm (× 10,000 for RTU). Normalise to DMSO control. Fit dose-response in GraphPad Prism v10 (bell-shaped or 4PL model depending on hook effect).
Tip
Ciulli lab: CoraFluor-1 (Tb cryptate from Payne & Mazitschek, 2022) coupled to Nano-Secondaries (single-domain anti-species nanobodies) offers superior performance vs. direct antibody labelling — eliminates batch-to-batch variability and works well in lysate-based assays. For binary target engagement (KD determination), use the competitive displacement format: pre-form binary complex at KD concentration, then titrate competing compound — more robust than direct binding for small molecules. See Payne et al. (2021) JACS for full CoraFluor characterisation.

Fluorescence Polarization (FP) — Binary Binding & Cooperativity

ReferenceBiophysics
What is this? Fluorescence polarization measures the rotational freedom of a small fluorescent molecule (tracer). When free in solution, a small fluorescent tracer tumbles rapidly → low polarization (mP). When bound to a large protein, rotation slows dramatically → high polarization. In a competitive FP assay, an unlabelled compound displaces the tracer from the protein, reducing polarization in a concentration-dependent manner, enabling IC50 and KD determination.
When to use it: FP is the workhorse assay for binary KD determination of PROTAC warheads to their E3 ligase (VHL or CRBN). Fast, cheap, no specialist instrumentation, works with standard fluorescence plate readers. For VHL: FAM-labelled HIF-1α peptide tracer + VCB complex. For CRBN: Cy5- or TAMRA-labelled thalidomide derivative + CRBN-DDB1. Also used to measure cooperativity α by comparing IC50 of the PROTAC against E3 with and without saturating concentrations of the POI.
Principle
  1. Excite tracer with plane-polarized light; measure emission in parallel (I∥) and perpendicular (I⊥) planes.
  2. Polarization mP = 1000 × (I∥ − I⊥) / (I∥ + I⊥). Bound state ~250–350 mP; free tracer ~20–50 mP. Dynamic range = mPbound − mPfree ≥ 150 mP preferred.
  3. Competition: titrate compound against pre-formed tracer–protein complex. IC50 converted to KD via Cheng–Prusoff: KD = IC50 / (1 + [tracer] / KD,tracer).
  4. Cooperativity α: run FP competition with and without saturating POI (e.g. BRD4 at ≥10× KD,PROTAC–BRD4). α = IC50,without POI / IC50,with POI. α > 1 = positive cooperativity.
VHL FP assay — reagents
  1. Tracer: FAM-labelled HIF-1α peptide. [Tracer] = 10–20 nM (at or below KD,tracer ~5–10 nM).
  2. Protein: VCB complex (VHL:ElonginC:ElonginB, His-tagged). Typical assay concentration: ~100–200 nM.
  3. Buffer: 25 mM HEPES pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl, 0.5 mM TCEP, 0.01% Tween-20. No BSA (can interfere with FP).
  4. Plates: black flat-bottom 96- or 384-well plates. Black walls minimise background fluorescence. 20–50 µL reaction volume.
VHL FP assay protocol
  1. Prepare compound serial dilutions (8–11 points, 3-fold, top 100–500 µM). Maximum 1% DMSO final. Run in duplicate or triplicate.
  2. Prepare protein-tracer mix: VCB at 2× final + FAM-tracer at 2× final in assay buffer. Pre-incubate 30 min on ice.
  3. Add 10 µL compound to wells; add 10 µL protein-tracer mix. Total 20 µL.
  4. Incubate 30–60 min at RT in the dark.
  5. Read FP: excitation 485 nm, emission 535 nm. Report in mP.
  6. Controls: tracer only (min FP), tracer + protein (max FP). Normalise: % inhibition = (mPsample − mPmin) / (mPmax − mPmin) × 100. Fit to sigmoidal dose-response for IC50.
  7. For KD: if [protein] ≥10× KD, use direct binding fit. Otherwise use Cheng-Prusoff correction.
CRBN FP assay — notes
  1. Tracer: Cy5- or TAMRA-labelled thalidomide/pomalidomide/lenalidomide derivative. [Tracer] = 10–50 nM.
  2. Protein: His-CRBN-DDB1 complex, 200–500 nM. Avoid CRBN-TBD alone — lower MW gives smaller dynamic range.
  3. IMiD positive controls: pomalidomide (KD ~1–5 µM), lenalidomide (~10–50 µM).
  4. Interfering compounds: check compound fluorescence at FAM/Cy5 excitation before running assay — inner filter effects are the most common artefact.
Cooperativity by FP
  1. Run standard FP competition assay for PROTAC vs. E3 ligase tracer.
  2. In parallel, add saturating POI (e.g. BRD4(BD1) at 10× KD,PROTAC-BRD4) to all wells; repeat.
  3. Cooperative ternary complex: IC50 shifts left. α = IC50,binary / IC50,ternary.
  4. Negative cooperativity: IC50 shifts right. Additive: unchanged.
Tip
FP is exquisitely sensitive to inner filter effects with coloured compounds — always measure compound absorbance at the excitation/emission wavelengths and include a reference well. Large PROTACs (>800 Da) can sometimes shift FP even when not specifically competing. Validate hits with an orthogonal method (ITC, SPR) before concluding on KD. The FAM-HIF1α/VCB FP assay is extremely well validated in the Ciulli lab — benchmark VHL warheads against VH032 (KD ~100 nM) and VH298 (KD ~80 nM) as positive controls.

SPR (Biacore) — Binary & Ternary Complex Kinetics

ReferenceBiophysics
What is this? Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR) is a label-free, real-time optical technique that detects changes in refractive index near a sensor chip surface. One protein is immobilised on the chip; binding partner flows over it and the instrument records association and dissociation in real time as a sensorgram (RU vs. time). SPR provides kinetic rate constants (kon, koff) and equilibrium affinity (KD = koff/kon) directly, without labels. For PROTACs, SPR enables the only direct measurement of ternary complex dissociation kinetics (koff,ternary) — a parameter that correlates with degradation rate. Pioneered for PROTACs by Roy, Winkler, Ciulli et al. (ACS Chem. Biol. 2019).
When to use it: mid-to-late campaign, when FP/TR-FRET have identified promising compounds and you need kinetic insight — especially when comparing compounds with similar Cmax but different cellular potency. Also essential for establishing structure-activity relationships for cooperativity. Requires Biacore instrument (T200, 8K, or 8K+), sufficient purified protein (>200 µg of E3 ligase for immobilisation), and careful surface chemistry.
SPR assay design — ternary complex
  1. Immobilised protein (ligand): E3 ligase component on chip. For VHL-based PROTACs: biotinylated VCB captured on Streptavidin chip (SA chip, Cytiva). Density: target 200–500 RU to minimise avidity effects and mass transfer limitations. Biotinylate using BirA enzyme and AviTag.
  2. Flowing analyte (binary): PROTAC alone (typically 0.1–10× KD, 6–8 concentrations, 3-fold dilutions). Use multi-cycle kinetics (MCK) for high-affinity binders (KD < 100 nM, slow koff). Use single-cycle kinetics (SCK) for fast-dissociating species.
  3. Flowing analyte (ternary): pre-form PROTAC:POI binary complex by pre-incubating PROTAC with saturating POI (e.g. 10 µM BRD4(BD1) with 1 µM PROTAC, 30 min on ice). This pre-formed complex is then flowed over the E3 chip.
  4. Cooperativity α by SPR: α = KD,binary / KD,ternary. α > 1 = positive cooperativity, α < 1 = negative, α = 1 = additive.
Running buffer & surface prep
  1. Running buffer: PBS-P+ (Cytiva) or 10 mM HEPES pH 7.4, 150 mM NaCl, 0.005% Tween-20, 0.5 mM TCEP. Degas and filter (0.22 µm). For small molecules: add 1–2% DMSO if needed (must calibrate DMSO correction curve).
  2. Reference surface: blank-captured or reference channel without protein, for double-referencing.
  3. DMSO correction: if compounds in DMSO, perform DMSO calibration (0.5–3% DMSO 7-point curve) and apply solvent correction — critical, or DMSO bulk refractive index causes false signal.
  4. Surface regeneration: for SA-captured biotinylated protein, typically no regeneration needed. If ternary complex dissociates slowly, short 10 mM glycine pH 1.5 pulse (5 s) or 1 M NaCl pulse restores surface; validate that regeneration does not reduce capacity.
Protocol — binary binding (PROTAC vs. immobilised VCB)
  1. Prime system with running buffer; condition chip with 3× buffer injections.
  2. Capture biotinylated VCB on SA chip: inject 10–50 nM VCB-biotin at 5 µL/min until desired RU reached (~300 RU). Block remaining SA sites with 3× 1 min biotin injections.
  3. Prepare PROTAC dilution series in running buffer (1% DMSO final). Typical: 1000, 333, 111, 37, 12, 4, 1.3 nM (7 points, 3-fold). Include blank injections for double-referencing.
  4. Inject each concentration: association 60–120 s, dissociation 120–300 s, flow rate 30–100 µL/min. Double-reference all data.
  5. Fit to 1:1 Langmuir model in Biacore Evaluation Software or Scrubber2. Extract kon, koff, KD.
Protocol — ternary complex (pre-formed PROTAC:POI vs. VCB)
  1. Pre-incubate PROTAC with saturating POI (e.g. 10 µM BRD4(BD1) + titrated PROTAC: 1000, 333, 111, 37, 12 nM) in running buffer, 30 min on ice.
  2. Use single-cycle kinetics: inject concentrations sequentially without regeneration (association 100 s per concentration, final dissociation 600–1200 s). Minimises surface stress.
  3. Include control: POI alone (same concentrations, no PROTAC) to confirm POI does not bind E3 in the absence of PROTAC.
  4. Double-reference and fit to 1:1 model. The ternary KD and koff,ternary are key outputs.
  5. Calculate α = KD,binary (PROTAC alone vs. VCB) / KD,ternary (PROTAC:POI vs. VCB).
Interpreting results in the degrader context
  1. koff,ternary predicts degradation rate: slower dissociation = longer ternary complex half-life = more time for E2 recruitment and ubiquitin transfer. MZ1 (BRD4BD2/VHL, α ~15–26) has t1/2,off ~60–90 s; correlates with faster, more complete BRD4 degradation vs. equipotent analogues with shorter ternary complex lifetimes.
  2. Hook effect visible in SPR: at very high [PROTAC], binary complexes dominate and less pre-formed PROTAC:POI binary complex is available → apparent RU drops at highest concentrations. Confirm this is hook effect (not artefact) by checking free PROTAC binding to E3 at same concentrations.
  3. Cooperativity α > 50: rare but powerful; seen for extremely well-packed ternary complexes. Target α ≥ 2–5 as a minimum for productive degradation; α > 10 for high-cooperativity compounds.
  4. Negative cooperativity (α < 1): PROTAC:POI binary complex binds E3 less well than PROTAC alone. Consider alternative exit vector, shorter/longer linker, or different E3 ligase. Not always fatal for degradation but worth addressing in SAR.
Tip
From the lab: the Ciulli lab protocol uses biotin-VCB immobilised on SA chip, multi-cycle kinetics for binary PROTAC binding, and single-cycle kinetics for ternary complex to avoid slow-off artefacts during regeneration. TCEP in buffer is critical for VHL (disulphide-sensitive). For CRBN-DDB1-based ternary SPR: His₆-CRBN-DDB1 can be captured via anti-His antibody surface, avoiding the need to chemically biotinylate. Some BRD4(BD1) PROTAC ternary complexes with VHL dissociate faster than the instrument's dead time; single-cycle kinetics with longer association windows partially mitigates this. Roy et al. 2019 ACS Chem. Biol. and the Ciulli lab Methods chapter (Springer Nature Protocols 2021) are the definitive references for this assay format.
